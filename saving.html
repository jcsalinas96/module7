How to Fund & Save for Your Trip

When you think of a vacation, images of sandy beaches, adventurous road trips, or exploring a new city might come to mind. I’m writing about how you can make those dreams happen—even if your budget is small. Because here’s the thing: the cost of travel is rising, yet many of us still want to get away. According to recent data, the average vacation cost for one person for a week in the U.S. is around $1,986. (Pacaso, 2025) If you’re not earning a windfall or saving for a luxury holiday, that number can feel daunting. In this article, I’ll walk you through concrete, realistic ways to save up for a vacation and fund it without derailing your broader financial goals.

Here’s one tactic: start a dedicated “vacation fund” and treat it like any other monthly expense. You could automate a small amount each paycheck into a savings account earmarked for your trip. Over 6–12 months, you’ll build a buffer so when it’s time to book flights or lodging you’re not relying totally on credit. Make smart choices like staying in an Airbnb or budget hotel, cooking some meals rather than eating out every time, driving instead of flying if time allows, and choosing off-season dates. These choices reduce your total required sum and make the goal feel more achievable.

Another key strategy is to use multiple income streams or side-hustles to "fund" the vacation separately from your everyday spending. For example, pick up a gig that you designate solely for travel savings, say selling handmade items, freelance work, or tutoring one evening a week. Because if your vacation savings come out of your regular “pay for bills + living expenses” pool, you’re more likely to dip into it for other things. Also, when you track how much you need (say you aim for $1,500 for a 4-day getaway), break it down: “I need $125/month for 12 months = $15/week.” That makes it feel far less intimidating. You’ll be surprised how fast small amounts add up.

At the end of the day, having a fantastic vacation on a modest budget isn't about cutting out fun, it’s about planning, intentional saving, smart spending, and choosing wisely. With some forward-thinking (and maybe a little side hustle grit) you can build the fund, lock in those deals, and enjoy that break without coming home to regret. Your next getaway could well be closer than it seems.
